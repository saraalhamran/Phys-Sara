Week 3: Hand Tools and Fabrication

Kinetic Sculpture — “Whale & Waves”

The brief: build a small kinetic sculpture using basic hand tools, laser-cut parts, and a simple motion mechanism. I set out to capture the calm rhythm of a whale riding waves—great motivation to learn cam-followers and linkages. I went in ambitious, learned tolerances the hard way, and finished with a working, soothing motion loop I’m proud of. (Shoutout to Mila Barry’s past projects for motor coupling inspo.)

At a glance
Motion:Cam + follower (elliptical cams)
Frame:Finger-jointed box (press-fit)
Drive:Motor → shaft coupler → main rod
Focus:Tolerances, spacing, smooth travel
Fix:Top plate swap to resolve binding

Design Intent

The sculpture is a finger-jointed box housing a rotating shaft. Elliptical cams on the shaft lift simple followers that animate the whale silhouette and layered wave panels. I aimed for an ocean-like rhythm instead of aggressive motion. Layout mattered more than expected: hole spacing, follower length, and cam offset all change the feel. I also underestimated how precisely the lid holes need to align with cam centers—being off by even a couple millimeters can bind the mechanism.

CAD & Planning

In Fusion, I parameterized material thickness and joint width to tweak kerf and fit without redrawing. I also modeled circular couplers to link the motor shaft to the main rod. The cams are ellipses—simple to cut and reliable for smooth rise/fall.

Assembly

I glued three sides first to create a stable base, then switched to wood glue for strength. I planned a wooden dowel but used a 5 mm metal rod that matched the design—bonus rigidity. The cams were glued to the shaft with rectangular followers riding on top. A gotcha: the top plate holes were slightly off relative to cam centers, causing binding. Fortunately I had a spare top; swapping it fixed the issue without re-cutting.

Mechanism Tuning

Most tuning time went into spacing: distance from cam center to follower hole, follower length/thickness, and slight phase differences between whale and waves. Small changes, big impact. I iterated until the whale felt fluid (not jerky) and the waves drifted slightly out of phase for depth.

Reflection

The mechanism wasn’t the hardest part—precision was. Hole placement, perpendicularity, and keeping things square mattered more than I expected. Next pass, I’ll add simple drill jigs for fool-proof spacing and lightly sand cam edges to reduce friction noise. Even with hiccups, the motion reads exactly how I hoped: simple, calm, and a little mesmerizing.
