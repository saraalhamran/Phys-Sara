Week 6: Electronic Inputs

Wireless Charger Sensor

Bobby pitched a great idea for my final project: use a wireless charger as both the sensor and the charger. The plan is to build a study helper robot that holds your phone while you focus. If the phone is on the pad and actually charging, the system starts the study timer. One part does double duty — neat and efficient.

I picked a Qi wireless charger and, since it was my first time using one, I started by mapping the pins. There are five inputs on the board. Using a multimeter, I measured voltages before and after placing a phone on the pad to find which pins actually changed with a real charging event.

I clipped the black probe to charger GND and tested each candidate pin with the red probe, logging the values. From those readings, I narrowed it down to the last two pins as good microcontroller inputs.

At the soldering station, we attached two leads: one to GND and one to the candidate pin. Getting GND to wet was surprisingly hard on two different boards; cranking the iron temperature higher finally did the trick. (Not always recommended, but it worked here.)

Pin C: the scope showed volatile spikes, peaking near 14 V — not ideal for a clean, safe signal path. We ruled it out.

Pin D: steady and predictable. Much better candidate for detection.

To protect the ESP32 and keep readings stable, I added a voltage divider (20 kΩ and 10 kΩ). That brings the signal into a safe range and smooths spikes. The rest of the circuit is straightforward: common ground, charger signal into an ESP32 ADC, and serial monitoring for calibration.
