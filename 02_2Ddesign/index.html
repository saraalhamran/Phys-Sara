Week 2: 2D Design & Cutting

This week was all about translating ideas into clean, cuttable geometry. I practiced parametric design, dialed in kerf for press-fit joints, and learned (the hard way) that “pretty shapes” still have to be manufacturable. Here’s the journey, with what worked, what didn’t, and what I’ll improve next time.

Test Download Button

Assignment 1: Make a Box

I came in swinging with ambitious “artsy” concepts… and immediately met geometry reality. Finger joints looked simple until I tried to make them parametric and kerf-aware. Huge thanks to Leo and the TAs for walking me through the right way to think about fit. I ended up shipping a simple press-fit box with clean tabs. It isn’t perfect—there’s a tiny gap I didn’t account for and one finger got clipped—but it’s solid progress and a good baseline for iteration.

What I learned
  • Make kerf a parameter. Update once, fix everywhere.
  • Square corners ≠ square assembly—check diagonals, not just edges.
  • Test joints in a small coupon before cutting the whole design.
Next iteration
  • Add small dog-bone fillets where tabs meet the walls.
  • Label faces in the cut file to speed up assembly.
  • Export with stroke weights the laser actually understands (hairline).

Assignment 2: Fusion 360 Tutorial

The tutorials were surprisingly fun. Modeling the LEGO brick made the Extrude, Shell, and Pattern tools click for me, and I started thinking in constraints instead of vibes. Getting the stud dimensions right also taught me how important references are when you’re replicating real-world parts.

Assignment 3: Fusion Modeling — AirPods Case

I wanted something I’d actually use, so I modeled an AirPods case. I got a nice first pass, then Fusion crashed and took some work with it (RIP unsaved timeline). Still, I’m happy with the form and the hinge clearances I was aiming for. The redo will be fully parametric with lid stop angles and wall thicknesses as named parameters, so I can try different materials later without redrawing everything.

Notes to future me
  • Save early, save often, and keep versions—Fusion can humble you.
  • Measure the real AirPods with calipers; don’t eyeball radii.
  • Draft angles matter if I ever injection-mold this (dream big).
If I laser-cut a prototype
  • Use living hinges for the lid on plywood/acrylic tests.
  • Add locating tabs so the halves register perfectly.
  • Line the inside with felt or TPU inserts to protect the case.

Reflection

The biggest mindset shift was going parametric and thinking in constraints. Once kerf, material thickness, and joint widths became variables, iterating got fast—and my cuts matched what I drew. Next up: bring these habits into 3D prints and start mixing laser-cut parts with simple printed features.
